How can design thinking change your entire life in a matter of 12 weeks?

A look back into my latest business design sprint

Of mind maps and humble beginnings..

I remember sitting in this same spot as I wrote my first ever reflection for this class. That day I learnt three very important things:

  • In the last 20 years, I had never been asked to think about my thinking process
  • Mind Maps are really useful tools
  • I am a very critical thinker

Now the first one might be surprising and the second one intuitive, it was the last observation that actually set the tone for the rest of my experience. You see, I knew I was a convergent thinker but it was only through self introspection did I acknowledge all the mental models I had adopted throughout my life.

The break in the case...

Needless to say, the course seemed quite challenging up to that point. I think my first real interaction with a learning insight came from the Ten Faces of Innovation. Whenever someone said innovation, I would think of an innovative digital breakthrough; it was intriguing to learn how innovation is all about the people - the roles they play, the knowledge they bring and the personas they can adopt.

I remember this class very well since it forced me to think about not just who I am but who I want to be. I am a critical thinker but does that stop me from being a storyteller or a cross-pollinator? How could I evolve into one or more of these faces of innovation?

“How will we solve a wicked problem like a housing challenge?!?!?!”

RBC’s question was simple but the answer to that question from the lens of user centered approach was quite complex.

Lean Ethnography, Interviews and Data Sorting...

My second key discoveries in the course were through the real time data research experience which taught me three things:

  • Observing random strangers for an extended period of time could get very frustrating but once I started observing with a fresh pair of eyes and through POEMS, I gained insights I would have missed otherwise. Ex. Observing at the Graduate Students’ Union pub, I was challenged in my notion that students are often careless with their money while drinking. I actually witnessed multiple students carefully taking out cash and coins and deliberating for a while before ordering the cheapest beer, leading me to believe that my confirmation bias was blocking my insights.
  • There is a difference between a problem and a need: Need highlights a pain point and can bring a human perspective to solving problems. Active observation helps us with understanding people’s work, family, beliefs and habits which allow us to find the human need.
  • Data Sorting is the process where it all makes sense for a brief moment, especially as my team got deeper insights into the emotional and functional reasons behind an average UTSG undergraduates’ habits and behaviors.

Feedback loops are everything… and I mean, everything…

Feedback loops are a constant stage of monitoring and testing. As a coder, this process of reiteration was probably the one I was most familiar with in the entire course but I really enjoyed seeing it come to live in context of a business design problem.
